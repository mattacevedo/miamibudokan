Getting started · June 21, 2026

Too old, too stiff, too busy? You can still start iaido

We have noticed something over the years. The people who hesitate longest before visiting are rarely worried about swords. They are worried about themselves. Too old, they tell us later, once they are happily training. Too out of shape, too inflexible, too busy.

We understand where the worries come from. Most people's mental image of a martial arts class involves twenty-year-olds doing jump kicks, and if that is the image, of course a reasonable adult hesitates. But that image has nothing to do with what happens on our floor, and since these three worries stop good people at the door, let us take them apart one at a time, thoroughly.

Too old

Iaido is famously an art people practice deep into life, and we mean deep. In Japan it is entirely normal to see practitioners training and testing in their seventies and eighties, and many of the art's most respected teachers did their finest work at ages when most sports have long since retired you. This is not a heartwarming exception. It is the ordinary shape of an iaido life.

There is a structural reason for it. Iaido has no sparring, no impacts, no falls, and nobody is throwing you. Rank comes from precision, understanding, and presence, not from athleticism, which means the qualities that fade with age are not the qualities the art runs on. Meanwhile the qualities that grow with age, patience, attention, the ability to practice something unglamorous without needing constant novelty, are exactly what iaido rewards. Experience is an actual advantage here, which makes this one of the rare physical pursuits where starting at fifty is not a compromise. It is arriving on time.

The art is also unusually kind to the long view. There is no peak to age out of. A practitioner's draw at seventy can genuinely be better than their draw at fifty, and working toward that is half the joy of the thing. Some of the best students we have ever had walked in convinced they were too late. They were right on time, and if you ask them now, they will tell you their only regret is the years spent hesitating.

Too stiff

You do not need to be flexible to begin. Read that again if you need to, because it stops more people than anything else. You need to be willing to move, and the art will gradually ask a little more of you, at your pace, over months and years, which is exactly how bodies like to be asked.

Iaido's movements are natural ones: stepping, turning, reaching, rising. There are no high kicks, no splits, and nothing acrobatic anywhere in the curriculum. The most demanding physical element for many adults is seiza, the formal kneeling posture some forms begin from, and here is the honest truth about that: knees vary, everyone knows knees vary, and the art has been accommodating this for generations. Standing variations exist for a reason. We adapt, cheerfully and without ceremony, and no one at our dojo will ever force your joints to prove a point. You will not be the only person in the room working around a body part. You may, in fact, be in the majority.

What tends to happen instead, quietly, over months, is that things improve. Posture straightens, because the art is built on posture. Balance steadies. Hips and shoulders loosen from the slow, repeated, careful movement, which is more or less what physical therapists prescribe anyway, except ours comes with swords and better outfits. We make no medical claims. We just watch it happen, year after year, to person after person.

Too busy

Here is our schedule, in full: Wednesday evenings from 8:30 to 9:30, Saturday afternoons from 1:00 to 3:00, and Sunday mornings for members' open practice. That is the entire commitment surface of this art. Nobody attends everything, and nobody is expected to.

Now the part that busy people need to hear most: a student who makes one class a week, most weeks, progresses beautifully. We have watched it over and over. This art is measured in years anyway, so consistency beats intensity every single time, and there is no falling behind because there is no pack to fall behind. Your practice is yours. The person next to you started a different year, tests on a different schedule, and struggles with different things. The only timeline that matters is whether this month's practice was a little more honest than last month's.

For a certain kind of overloaded adult, and Miami produces this kind of adult in quantity, one guarded hour a week turns out to be the most valuable hour on the calendar. It is the hour nobody can email you. Far from being a scheduling burden, it becomes the thing holding the rest of the week upright. One good hour a week is not a compromise. It is a practice, and there is a long tradition of exactly that.

The real requirement

There is only one, and we did not invent it, we inherited it: a willingness to learn. That is the actual entrance criterion. Not youth, not fitness, not free time, not talent. A willingness to be a beginner, to be corrected kindly, and to come back.

That is also why our dojo is committed, explicitly, to welcoming every student, of every age, background, and body, without exception. It is on our website and it is in our practice, and the room itself is the proof: on any given Saturday our floor holds a wider range of ages and bodies than almost any gym in this city, all doing the same art, each at their own pace.

Your first class is free, and sweatpants are perfect. Reach out, tell us you are coming, and come find out that the person you were worried about was never the person who showed up.
