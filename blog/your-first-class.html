Getting started · February 15, 2026

Your first iaido class, from the parking lot to the final bow

Trying anything for the first time is a little nerve-wracking. Trying a martial art, in a room full of people holding swords, can feel like a lot. So let us walk you through exactly what your first visit looks like, start to finish, with nothing left mysterious.

We have welcomed a lot of first-timers over the years, and we can tell you the single most common thing they say afterward: "that was much less intimidating than I expected." Our goal with this post is to get you to that feeling before you even arrive.

Before you come

Send us a note or give us a call first. This is partly dojo custom and partly practical, since we like to know a guest is coming so someone can greet you properly at the door. Nobody should stand in a doorway wondering if they are in the right place.

Your first class is free, and you have two completely acceptable options: watch from the side, or step onto the floor and try it. Tell us which way you are leaning when you write, and feel free to change your mind when you arrive. Some people know instantly they want to try. Others want to sit with a cup of curiosity and just observe. Both are honored guests, and nobody will pressure you either way. We mean that. We are a not-for-profit dojo, and there is no sales script here, because there is nothing to sell you beyond what you can see with your own eyes.

What to wear

Comfortable athletic clothing. Sweatpants and a t-shirt are honestly perfect. You will be barefoot on a wood floor, so no special shoes are needed, and jewelry is best left at home since it can catch on things and scratch equipment.

You do not need to buy anything, and please, we say this with love, do not show up with a sword you found online. Beginners work with wooden practice swords, and we will take care of what you need for your first class. The equipment conversation comes later, slowly, with guidance, and it will save you from the internet's enthusiasm for selling sharp objects to beginners.

When you arrive

Come a few minutes early so you are not rushing. You will see people bow as they enter the training floor. You can simply nod politely, and someone will show you the basics of etiquette before anything else. Nobody expects a first-time visitor to know the customs. Teaching them to you kindly is, in a real sense, part of the art, and we wrote a whole post about why the bowing matters if you want a head start.

You will meet whoever is teaching that day, and probably half the room besides. Iaido people are, as a group, surprisingly chatty before and after class for practitioners of a silent art. Someone will point you to where you can put your things, and someone will make sure you always know what is happening next.

The first hour, in detail

Class begins with a formal bow-in, a short moment of stillness, and warm-ups. The warm-ups are simple: joints, shoulders, wrists, some basic swinging movements to wake up the body. Nobody is doing splits. Nobody is doing burpees. The most demanding thing about iaido warm-ups is that they reveal how interesting it is to simply swing your arms with full attention.

Then come fundamentals. If you have joined the floor, a senior student or instructor will stay near you and start you on the true basics: how to stand, how to hold the wooden sword, how to take a step without wobbling, how to raise the sword overhead and bring it down in a straight, unhurried line. That last one will occupy you for a surprisingly enjoyable long time. Meanwhile, the rest of the class works on their own forms around you, which means you get to watch the art at every level, from fellow beginners to teachers, all in one room.

Expect simple things done carefully. Expect to be corrected, early and often, and to discover that the corrections feel like gifts rather than criticism. That is the culture here, and honestly it is the culture of iaido generally. Everyone in the room was a beginner once, and this art is humbling enough at every level that nobody has room for arrogance.

Expect also to feel slightly awkward. Everyone does. The senior students you will watch gliding through forms felt exactly the same way at their first class, and most of them will happily tell you about it, in detail, with laughing.

Afterward

Class ends the way it began, with a bow and a moment of quiet. Then the room relaxes, and this is your moment to ask us anything. Fees, schedule, uniforms, how long things take, whether your knees will survive (they will, and we adapt for them), what that one movement was called, why the swords are different lengths, anything at all.

If iaido is for you, you will feel it, usually somewhere between the second and tenth time you draw a sword with your whole attention. And then you simply come back next week. Fees are month to month with nothing to sign, so the decision stays exactly as light as it should be.
