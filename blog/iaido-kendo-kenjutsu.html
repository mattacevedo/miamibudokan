The basics · April 19, 2026

Iaido, kendo, or kenjutsu? An honest guide to picking your sword art

If you have been googling Japanese sword arts, you have met three words that look interchangeable and are not: iaido, kendo, and kenjutsu. Websites tend to explain them with history lectures. We are going to explain them with experiences, because what you actually want to know is what your Tuesday nights would feel like in each one.

And we will give you honest advice about which might fit you, even when the answer is not us. Miami has room for every kind of swordsman, and we would rather you find the right art than simply the nearest one.

Kendo, the fighting one

Kendo is Japanese fencing. You wear armor, called bogu, you hold a bamboo sword called a shinai, and an opponent is actively, energetically trying to score on you while you try to score on them. Points come from clean strikes to defined targets, delivered with correct form, timing, and full spirit, which in practice means kendo is loud. There is shouting. It is required, actually, and it is glorious.

Kendo is fast, competitive, and a serious cardiovascular workout. A hard practice will leave you soaked and grinning. Tournaments are a big part of the culture, and the art has a worldwide competitive structure, so a kendo player in Miami can measure themselves against anyone.

What does a kendo person tend to be like? Someone who wants an opponent. Someone energized by pressure, speed, and the electric moment where two people commit at once. If reading that gave you a little jolt of excitement, pay attention to that jolt.

Several of our instructors hold kendo rank alongside their iaido rank and love the art dearly. Better still, we can point you in exactly the right direction: our partner dojo, Toku Bu Kan, teaches kendo in our very same building, with classes on Tuesdays and Thursdays at the Japan Cultural Center. Same address, different nights, different art. If kendo is your fit, visit them and tell them we sent you. We consider that a good outcome, not a lost prospect.

Iaido, the one we teach

Iaido is the art of drawing the sword. Every form starts with the blade sheathed and ends with it returned. There is no sparring, no armor, and no opponent except the one you imagine, which turns out to be the most persistent opponent of all: yourself.

The work is precision, posture, timing, and a calm mind, refined through solo forms practiced for a lifetime. A single kata might contain a draw that must arrive at exactly the right angle, a cut that must fall in a perfectly straight line, a pause whose length is itself part of the technique, and a resheathing performed with complete control while your attention stays extended. Every detail is visible. Nothing is hidden by speed or chaos. This is either maddening or captivating, and which one it is for you is basically the whole aptitude test.

Kendo is a sprint against an opponent. Iaido is a conversation with yourself. People who love iaido tend to love depth, detail, and the feeling of a distracted mind going quiet. They tend to be people who enjoy doing one thing extremely well rather than many things quickly. It also happens to be easy on the body, no impacts, no falls, no one hitting you, which is why people practice it into their seventies and beyond, and why plenty of practitioners take it up in midlife after harder arts have finished with their knees.

One more distinction worth knowing: because there is no opponent to defeat, progress in iaido is measured by rank examinations and by the daily evidence of your own practice. If you need a scoreboard to stay motivated, kendo will feed you better. If you find scoreboards slightly beside the point, welcome home.

Kenjutsu, the old umbrella

Kenjutsu is a broad term for classical combat swordsmanship, the techniques of the old schools as they existed before modern Japan reorganized its martial arts. It is usually practiced as paired forms, two partners with wooden swords moving through prearranged sequences of attack and response, in schools whose lineages stretch back centuries.

In practice, the line between kenjutsu and iaido is blurry, and here is the part most beginners do not know: classical schools live inside many iaido dojo, including ours. Alongside the modern standardized curriculum, we practice Muso Shinden-ryu, a classical school passed down teacher to student for generations. So if the deep history is what draws you, the old forms and the sense of receiving something ancient, iaido is a genuine doorway into exactly that. You do not have to choose between the living tradition and the modern art. At our dojo you practice both, and each one improves the other.

How to actually choose

Our advice is unglamorous: go watch. Watching costs nothing and settles more than any amount of reading.

Watch a kendo practice at Toku Bu Kan some Tuesday and notice whether the intensity thrills you or exhausts you. Then come watch our iaido class and notice whether the focus bores you or pulls you in. Be honest about what you observe in yourself, not about what you think you should prefer. The person who is a little disappointed that iaido looks quiet should probably try kendo. The person who watches a single slow, perfect draw and feels something settle in their chest should probably sit down, because that is the hook going in, and we have seen it happen from the visitor bench many times.

One more honest note: plenty of people are suited to more than one of these arts, and some practice more than one across a lifetime. This is not a marriage. It is a first class.
