Muso Shinden-ryu, the classical school we practice

Most weeks at our dojo, you will see two kinds of practice happening on the same floor. One is the modern standardized iaido curriculum, the seitei forms shared by practitioners around the world. The other is something older, with a different flavor entirely. It is called Muso Shinden-ryu, and it is one of our favorite things to teach.

This post is a small introduction to it: what a classical school actually is, where this one comes from, and why practicing something old changes how you understand everything else.

What a koryu is

Koryu means old school, or ancient style. These are the classical Japanese traditions that were shaped before Japan's modern era and survive the only way they can, by being handed from teacher to student, one generation at a time, with the details intact.

Think about what that means practically. You cannot learn a koryu from a video, because the video cannot correct you. You cannot certify yourself in one, because the school is not a syllabus, it is a living chain of people. Every legitimate teacher of a classical school received it from someone who received it from someone, all the way back. This is why we talk about lineage the way musicians talk about who they studied under, and why the martial arts world takes such care about who is authorized to teach what.

A koryu is, in the most literal sense, an endangered cultural artifact that stays alive only through practice. Every class where the old forms are done correctly is an act of preservation, the same way a chamber ensemble playing a centuries-old score keeps that music alive. Miss a generation, and it is gone.

Where Muso Shinden-ryu comes from

The school descends from the tradition of drawing the sword that grew from the Hayashizaki line, named for the sixteenth-century swordsman credited with founding the systematic study of the draw. Over the following centuries that tradition branched, each branch shaped by the teachers who carried it.

The school as we practice it was founded in the early twentieth century by Nakayama Hakudo, one of the most celebrated swordsmen of his era, a man who studied the older branches deeply and arranged their teaching into the structured form that now bears the name Muso Shinden-ryu. So the school is at once genuinely old, its forms and principles carrying centuries of refinement, and coherently organized for transmission, which is part of why it became one of the most widely practiced classical iaido traditions in the world.

The shape of the curriculum

Muso Shinden-ryu is commonly taught in three levels, and the progression itself is a piece of design worth admiring.

The first level, called shoden, the beginning transmission, is practiced mostly from seiza, the formal kneeling posture. These forms teach the fundamentals: the draw, the cut, the cleaning of the blade, the return, all from a position that gives you nowhere to hide. Kneeling strips away footwork tricks and forces the body to learn its real lessons early.

The middle transmission, chuden, moves to a different starting posture and a different energy. The forms grow more fluid and more demanding of the legs and hips, and the scenarios widen.

The deep transmission, okuden, comes last, after years, with forms that are faster, subtler, and more varied, including practice from walking and responses to trouble arriving from awkward directions. A student meets each level in order, over years, the way the school intends. There is no skipping ahead, and truthfully, nobody who understands the progression wants to skip ahead. Each level makes the next one make sense.

How it differs from the modern forms

The seitei curriculum is precise, uniform, and shared worldwide, which is exactly its virtue. Judges in Miami and judges in Tokyo are looking for the same things, and your rank means the same thing everywhere. It is the common language of modern iaido.

The koryu forms feel different in the body. They carry the assumptions of an older world, closer quarters, other postures, other kinds of trouble, and they permit a little more individual character in the performance. Practicing them is like reading an old text in the original language instead of in translation. Suddenly you see where the modern forms came from, why they are shaped the way they are, and what was smoothed out in the standardizing.

The two practices feed each other constantly. The old forms explain the modern ones and deepen them. The modern forms sharpen the precision you bring back to the old ones. Students who practice both, which at our dojo is simply how we train, tend to understand each side better than they would understand either alone.

Why it matters that it lives here

There is something quietly remarkable about the fact that an unbroken classical tradition of Japanese swordsmanship is practiced every week in Coral Terrace, Miami, between the traffic and the palm trees. We do not take it for granted.

Preserving these arts, accurately and respectfully, is the core of our mission as a not-for-profit dojo. It is why our instructors hold certified ranks and teaching credentials rather than self-declared titles, and why we maintain the connection to the art's home in Japan through the federation and through our teachers. A tradition like this is not ours to modify for convenience. It is ours to receive carefully and hand on intact, hopefully having polished our small stretch of the chain.

You do not need to know any of this history to begin, and beginners start with the fundamentals either way, the same stance, the same first cut, the same patient corrections. But if the idea of receiving something old, real, and carefully kept appeals to you, if you are the kind of person who touches a two-hundred-year-old building with a little reverence, come watch a Saturday class. The old school is alive and well, and it is closer than you think.
