Etiquette · March 8, 2026

Why we bow

Almost every visitor asks about it eventually, usually a little carefully, the way you ask about a custom you do not want to offend. What is with all the bowing?

It is a great question, and we love answering it, because the bowing is not decoration. It might be the most useful thing we teach, and we say that as people who teach swordsmanship.

What the bow is

A bow in the dojo is three things at once. It is a greeting, it is a thank you, and it is a small announcement to yourself that your attention has arrived somewhere.

We bow when we enter the training floor and when we leave it. We bow toward the front of the room, called the shomen, which holds the place of honor and represents everything the art owes to the people who carried it before us. We bow to our teachers and training partners at the start and end of practice. And we even bow to the sword itself, before we pick it up and after we set it down.

That last one surprises people, so let us explain it. Bowing to the sword is not sword worship. It is an acknowledgment of what you are about to handle: an object that demands care, respect, and full attention, and a tradition that took centuries to reach your hands. The bow marks the boundary between casual handling and practice. On one side of that bow, it is an object on a rack. On the other side, it is the center of your attention for an hour.

What the bow is not

It is not worship, and it is not submission. Nobody at our dojo is pretending to be a feudal retainer, and no one is collecting loyalty. When you bow to a teacher, you are saying thank you for the correction I am about to receive. When they bow back, and they always bow back, they are saying thank you for trusting me with your practice. It runs in both directions, every single time, from the newest beginner to the most senior teacher in the room.

If you have ever felt uneasy about the idea of bowing, we understand, truly. Americans are not raised with it, and from the outside it can look like hierarchy theater. From the inside it feels completely different. It feels like mutual respect with a physical form, which turns out to be far more durable than respect that only lives in words.

What it actually does

Here is the part we find hard to explain until people feel it. The bow works like a doorway. On one side of it is your day, the traffic on Bird Road, the phone, the list of things you did not finish. On the other side is one hour where your only job is the next movement.

That transition does not happen by itself. Anyone who has tried to meditate at home knows that simply deciding to be present does not make it so. The bow makes it physical. Stand at the edge of the floor, pause, bow, and something in you actually shifts. The posture of respect produces the attitude of respect, not the other way around. Traditional arts figured this out centuries before psychology got around to writing papers about it.

After a few weeks you will notice you have stopped thinking about the bow and started relying on it. Many longtime practitioners will tell you the bow at the beginning of practice is the moment their week actually resets, more than the weekend, more than anything.

There is a practical layer too, and it matters. A room where people handle swords, even wooden ones, needs shared habits of care and acknowledgment. The etiquette tells everyone where attention belongs, who is moving where, and when. Etiquette is how a dojo stays both warm and safe at the same time. When courtesy is the room's default setting, correction never feels like attack, and nobody gets surprised while holding something long and pointed.

The etiquette beyond the bow

Bowing is the visible part of a larger fabric called reishiki, the etiquette of the dojo. It includes small things: how to carry a sword when walking through the room, how to sit while watching, how to receive a correction. None of it is complicated, and all of it gets taught to you patiently, piece by piece, as it becomes relevant. Nobody hands you a rulebook. The dojo simply shows you, the way a good host shows a guest around a home.

And that is really the spirit of it. Etiquette in a dojo is hospitality with structure. It is how forty generations of teachers made sure their training halls stayed places of respect rather than places of ego.

You will not be graded on it

First-time visitors sometimes worry about getting the customs wrong, as if there were a hidden exam. Please do not. We will show you everything, no one expects polish, and a sincere nod on day one counts just fine. The forms of respect are things you grow into, not things you are tested on at the door. Getting them wrong as a beginner is not rude. It is expected, and correcting you kindly is a job the whole room takes quiet pleasure in.

Come see it for yourself some Wednesday evening or Saturday afternoon. Just let us know you are coming. We will be the ones bowing, and within a visit or two, so will you, without even thinking about it. That is how it works. That is rather the whole point.
