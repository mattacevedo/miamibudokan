The basics · January 25, 2026

What is iaido? A plain-English introduction to the art of the Japanese sword

When we tell people what we practice, we usually get one of two reactions. Either "you practice what now?" or "wait, like samurai stuff?"

Both are fair. So here is the honest answer, in plain language, from the people who do it every week.

Iaido (pronounced ee-eye-doh) is the traditional Japanese art of drawing the sword. Each form begins with the sword still in its scabbard. You draw, you cut, you clear the blade, and you return it. Then you do it again, a little better.

That might sound simple. It is not. It is, however, wonderfully learnable, and that combination is the whole appeal.

What the word actually means

The name is made of three ideas. The "i" (居) carries the sense of being, of presence, of existing right where you are. The "ai" (合) means harmony or meeting. And "do" (道) means the way, the same "do" you find in judo (柔道) and kendo (剣道) and calligraphy's shodo (書道). Put together, iaido (居合道) is something like "the way of being present and ready." The sword is the instrument, but presence is the subject.

You will notice what is missing from that name: there is no word for fighting, winning, or opponent. That absence is not an accident, and it tells you most of what makes this art different from what people imagine when they hear "sword class."

What a practice actually looks like

If you walked into our dojo on a Saturday afternoon, here is what you would see: a bright room with a wood floor and a wall of mirrors, and a handful of people in dark uniforms moving through forms called kata. Some are beginners using wooden swords. Some are senior students using steel practice blades. Everyone is working on the same thing, which is doing one movement completely, with full attention.

A kata in iaido is a short, complete scenario. You are seated, or standing, or walking. Something happens, an imagined threat from the front, the side, behind you. You draw and respond, finish cleanly, and return the sword to the scabbard with control.

Every kata is built from the same handful of stages, in the same order, no matter which one you are practicing. First is nukitsuke, the draw itself, which doubles as the first cut, the sword leaving the scabbard already on its way to meeting the threat. Then come any finishing cuts, kirioroshi and the like, delivered with the whole body, not just the arms. After the threat is resolved comes chiburi, a controlled motion that symbolically clears the blade. Last is noto, the slow, deliberate return of the sword to the scabbard without looking down at your own hand, which turns out to be far harder than it sounds. Our curriculum holds several dozen of these kata, arranged roughly from simple to demanding, and a student will spend years moving through them.

Most kata take well under a minute to perform. Most take years to perform well. Nobody is bothered by this ratio. It is rather the point: the form does not change much once you have learned its shape, but what you notice inside it keeps changing, for as long as you keep showing up.

There is no sparring in iaido. Your opponent is imagined, which means your real opponent is yourself. Your habits, your hurry, your wandering mind. That is not a poetic flourish. It is the actual curriculum, and you will feel it about ten minutes into your first class, when you discover how much of your attention leaks away from even a simple movement.

Class time also includes the quieter machinery of a traditional art: the etiquette of entering and leaving the floor, the care of the sword, the unhurried repetition of fundamentals. None of it is busywork. All of it is part of how the art teaches.

Where it comes from

The art of drawing the sword goes back centuries in Japan. Tradition traces it to the swordsmen of the sixteenth century who studied a specific and rather practical problem: what happens in the instant before a fight begins, when the sword is still sheathed and everything depends on composure. Over hundreds of years, schools formed around this study, refined it, and passed it from teacher to student in unbroken lines.

We teach two connected things at our dojo. The first is the modern standardized curriculum of the Zen Nippon Kendo Renmei, usually called seitei iaido, which is practiced the same way around the world and is used for rank testing through the All United States Kendo Federation. The second is Muso Shinden-ryu, a classical school with much older roots, which we practice alongside the modern forms. If those names mean nothing to you yet, that is completely fine. They will, soon enough, and we wrote a whole post about each.

Why people stay

People come to iaido for the swords. We will not pretend otherwise, and honestly, the swords deserve the attention. A well-made blade is a beautiful object, and learning to handle one safely and precisely is deeply satisfying.

But people stay for what the swords teach. An hour of practice asks for your complete attention, and gives you back a quiet mind. There are very few places left in modern life where you cannot check your phone, where nobody wants anything from you except presence, and where the measure of success is simply: was that cut a little more honest than the last one?

The physical benefits arrive quietly. Posture improves, because the art is obsessive about posture. Balance improves. A certain unhurried steadiness shows up in how you move. And the mental benefits follow the same schedule: patience, focus, the ability to stay calm while doing something difficult. That last one follows you home, into traffic, into meetings, into every part of life that tests your composure.

Who this is for

Adults of any age, students, retirees, athletes, and people who have never done anything athletic in their lives. Iaido has no sparring, no falling, and no contact, which makes it one of the most accessible martial arts there is. It rewards attention rather than aggression, and consistency rather than intensity. Some of the finest practitioners in the world are in their seventies and eighties.
